The Decline of a Giant: Gray Whales in a Changing Ocean

Gray whale populations are rapidly declining as climate change and shifting ocean ecosystems threaten one of conservation’s greatest success stories.

By: Keegan Oates | May 13th, 2026

Gray whales were once a conservation success story. Today, their population is rapidly declining, and scientists are racing to understand why.

Nearly 50 years after the United States banned commercial whaling, some whale species, particularly gray whales, experienced a sharp population decline, with numbers dropping by nearly 50% over the past decade, an event classified as an unusual mortality event by the National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration.

Scientists believe climate change is a major factor as warmer oceans and melting Arctic ice are reducing amphipods, the tiny crustaceans gray whales rely on for food, which are unable to survive in these harsher oceanic conditions.

This has led gray whale populations to travel greater distances to find food, leaving them with less energy to reproduce. This is reflected in the significant decrease in the number of calves produced over the last decade.

“1,000s of whales died. That was just not even in the realm of things that we imagined.”

With this said, some other whale populations have been able to thrive and continue to grow, including humpback whales, which saw an almost immediate resurgence in population growth following whaling issues of the early to mid-1990s.

This has led researchers around the world, including Dr. Ted Cheeseman, to devote their careers to the issue. He is a co-founder and current director of Happy Whale, an organization that combines citizen scientists with researchers to create a whale information system used globally.

“We’re basically very confident now that if we have a good quality photo of a whale, we can either match it or visually confirm it,” said Cheeseman.

This new technology has been huge for protecting humpback whales and documenting accurate scientific data to track changes in their populations.

The technology only covers humpbacks for now, as their features are easier to detect via imagery. This leaves uncertainty when it comes to the correct number of gray whales along the San Luis Obispo central coast, where fewer calves are being spotted migrating toward Baja California.

“Creating awareness, for me, is really where Happy Whale is. That’s what we’re about,” said Cheeseman.

Along with this pressing matter, Cheeseman recently received his Ph.D. after studying a third of Hawaii’s gray whales that suddenly disappeared from their usual migration path. Researchers initially believed the shift was due to changes in food sources.

With further research, they discovered these whales had died due to marine heat waves that hit Hawaii’s oceans between 2012 and 2016, something previously thought to be unlikely.

Local whale watching business owner Mark Kocina has also seen the impact firsthand.

“We basically used to have a thriving business from December through April. I no longer have much business for them.”

Kocina, who formerly worked at the Monterey Bay Aquarium, believes gray whale populations are starving, contributing to their decline.

He also suggests there may be additional factors beyond climate change. He points to disruptions in the food chain caused by commercial fishing, particularly bluefin tuna migration patterns affecting sardine populations.

This food disruption has been evident recently, as a record number of 21 gray whales have washed up in San Francisco Bay since 2025, likely due to exhaustion and lack of food.

As concerns grow, researchers like Cheeseman believe it may be time to return gray whales to the endangered species list.

“Gray whales are the original success story... except now they actually need to go back on.”

Despite these concerns, Cheeseman remains optimistic, noting that other whale populations have recovered after similar declines. He believes there is still hope that gray whales can return to population levels seen as recently as 2016.
